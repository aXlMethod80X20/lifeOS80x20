## 01 — LinkedIn Post

*Angle: Thought leadership. Banking colleagues will see this.*

---

The Greeks had a word for it: *eidos*.

The invisible form that gives structure to everything you perceive.

Plato argued reality runs on these forms — that what we see are imperfect copies of something more fundamental.

Aristotle turned that into a practical system. He catalogued the basic categories of being: substance, quantity, quality, relation. He was asking: what are the irreducible patterns underneath all of this?

That's what Charlie Munger spent 60 years building — except he called it a latticework.

"You've got to have models in your head. And you've got to array your experience — both vicarious and direct — on this latticework of models."

Howard Marks added the missing layer: cycles. Time. Where you are in the distribution of outcomes matters more than what the average outcome is.

Both of them are pointing at the same thing:

**The map is not the territory. But you will die without a map.**

I've been thinking about this across three domains simultaneously:
→ Mental clarity — building the latticework, actually using it
→ Physical consistency — the body as a long-term compounding machine
→ Capital allocation — the barbell, not the bell curve

I built a small tool that maps all three. One HTML file. Open source. No login, no subscription, no cloud.

It's called lifeOS80x20. It's for people running a demanding job, training seriously, and building something on the side — without letting any of it collapse.

The philosophy is simple: 80% in the safe, boring, predictable lane. 20% in the genuinely asymmetric bet. Nothing in the middle. The middle is where fragility hides dressed as stability.

Munger knew it. Marks knows it. Taleb formalized it.

I just tried to make it usable on a Tuesday morning.

## 02 — Instagram Post

*Angle: Mental models + ontology (Greek history) + convex operator. Educational/punchy.*

---

Mental models are just ontology with better PR.

The Greeks invented this 2,500 years ago.

Plato called the underlying patterns *eidos* — the invisible forms that everything in reality imperfectly copies. Aristotle went further and built a full taxonomy of existence: substance, quantity, quality, relation, place, time.

They were doing exactly what Munger does. They were asking:

**What are the irreducible categories of how the world actually works?**

Munger's latticework is a practical ontology:
→ Inversion — start from the failure, work backwards
→ Circle of Competence — know the boundary of your actual edge
→ Second-Order Thinking — ask "and then what?" one more time than everyone else
→ Margin of Safety — price your own ignorance into every decision

Howard Marks adds the temporal layer. Markets move in cycles. Your edge isn't prediction — it's knowing where in the distribution of outcomes you currently are.

Now add the **convex operator**.

A function f is convex when:

f(average x) < average(f(x))

Translation: you benefit *more* from variability than the midpoint would suggest. Upside is not capped symmetrically with downside.

The barbell strategy is convex by design.
80% safe, linear, boring.
20% asymmetric — where optionality lives.

You don't need to predict what happens next.
You just need a payoff structure that gains from being surprised.

The Greeks were building ontologies.
Munger built a latticework.
Taleb formalized the convexity.

They were all doing the same thing:

*Mapping the territory so the map doesn't kill you.*
